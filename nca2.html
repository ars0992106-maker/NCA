1 FOUNDATIONS

Non-Current Assets

Your bakery buys flour — it's gone within a week. It also buys an oven — that stays for years. The oven is a non-current asset.

Definition

Non-current assets (NCAs) = assets held longer than one year to help the business earn money. They are not intended to be turned into cash quickly.

Examples: 🚐 Motor vehicles · 🏢 Premises · 🖥️ Equipment · ⚙️ Machinery · 🪑 Furniture

The big question: if an NCA helps you earn money for 5 years, should you record its entire cost as an expense in Year 1? That feels unfair. This is why we need depreciation — but first, let's figure out what "cost" actually means.

What Goes Into the "Cost" of an NCA?

Under the historical cost principle, assets are recorded at their actual acquisition cost. But not every dollar spent around an asset counts. We split all spending into two categories:

2 FOUNDATIONS

Capital vs Revenue Expenditure

💎 Capital Expenditure 資本支出

Long-term benefit. Added to the NCA cost on the SFP.

"This spending creates or improves something lasting."

🔥 Revenue Expenditure 收益支出

Short-term benefit only. Expensed in the Income Statement now.

"This spending is used up — it won't benefit next year."

Case 1: Buying a NEW NCA

Capital = purchase price + anything needed to bring the NCA to its location and condition for use.

The Golden Rule

Ask: "Without this spending, could the asset actually be used?"
NO → Capital. YES → Revenue.

Revenue clues: repeating payments (annual insurance), consumables (petrol, electricity).

Case 2: Spending on EXISTING NCAs

  • Extends useful life

Case 3: General Services

Rent, salaries, utilities → always revenue expenditure. Not related to creating/improving any NCA.

Repairs on a NEWLY-BOUGHT second-hand asset

Repairing a broken engine before first use → CAPITAL (needed to bring it to working condition).

Repairing a breakdown during daily use → Revenue (just restoring it).

Insurance

Insurance during transit of new asset → CAPITAL (without it, delivery won't happen).

Annual insurance during normal use → Revenue (machine works fine without it).

3 FOUNDATIONS

Why Depreciation?

You buy a van for $60,000, useful for 5 years. If you expense it all in Year 1:

  • Year 1: profit looks terrible (huge expense, but van only helped part of the time)
  • Years 2–5: you're using the van for free in your accounts. Not fair!
Matching Principle 相配原則

Expenses must be matched to the same period as the revenue they generate. Van helps earn money for 5 years → spread its cost over 5 years.

Definition

Depreciation = the systematic allocation of the depreciable amount of an NCA over its estimated useful life.

Plain English: spreading the "used up" cost across all the years the asset works for you.

What Causes It?

  1. Physical wear & tear — gets old from use
  2. Obsolescence — new technology makes it outdated
  3. Inadequacy — business grows, asset is no longer enough
Depreciation ≠ market value drop. A building may rise in market value, but we still depreciate it — we're allocating cost, not tracking value.

TermMeaning

5 METHODS

Straight-Line Method

Concept

Asset generates benefits evenly → charge the same depreciation every year. Like cutting a pizza into equal slices.

Formulas
Annual Dep = (Cost − Residual Value) ÷ Useful Life
Annual Dep = (Cost − Residual Value) × Rate
Rate = 100% ÷ Useful Life (e.g. 5 years → 20%)
Exam clue: "X% on cost" = straight-line method.

Example 1: Life & Residual Given

1 Jan 2022: Motor vehicle $6,500. Life: 5 years. Scrap: $500. Straight-line. Year end: 31 Dec.

Annual dep = ($6,500 − $500) ÷ 5 = $1,200

6 METHODS

Reducing-Balance Method

Concept

Apply the rate to the net book value (not cost) each year. Since NBV shrinks, depreciation gets smaller every year.

Formula
Depreciation = (Cost − Acc. Dep.) × Rate = NBV × Rate

IGNORE the residual value in the calculation. Just apply rate × NBV. The residual value is NOT subtracted from cost first (unlike straight-line).

Exam clue: "X% on net book value" or "on reducing/diminishing balance" = this method.

Worked Example

Machine $8,000. 1 Jan 2020. Residual: $1,000. Rate: 20% on NBV.

7 METHODS

Usage-Based Method

Concept

Depreciation based on actual usage (units produced, hours, km) rather than time.

Formula
Dep = (Cost − Residual) × (Actual units this year ÷ Total estimated units)

Machine $12,000. Residual $2,000. Total output: 200,000 units.

Time Apportionment

Asset bought mid-year? The exam will tell you which policy to use:

Policy A: Pro-rata (Monthly Basis)

Charge proportionally: annual dep × months available / 12

Bought 1 April, year end 31 Dec → 9/12

Policy B: Full Year in Acquisition

Full depreciation in year of purchase. None in year of disposal.

Straight-Line + Time

Machinery $120,000. 1 April 2020. Residual $12,000. Rate: 25% on cost. Year end: 31 Dec.

Full annual dep = ($120,000 − $12,000) × 25% = $27,000

Special Cases

A. From Given Balances

Given: Premises $120,000. Acc dep $24,000.

(a) 20% on cost → $120,000 × 20% = $24,000

(b) 10% on NBV → ($120,000 − $24,000) × 10% = $9,600

B. Multiple Groups (Different Purchase Dates)

Calculate separately in the year with different history, then pool together afterwards.

Van A: $72,000 on 1 Apr 2020. Van B: $45,000 on 1 Jul 2020. Rate: 20% on cost, pro-rata.

YearVan AVan BTotal
2020$72K×20%×9/12$45K×20%×6/12$15,300
2021+($72K+$45K)×20% = same$23,400

For reducing balance with multiple groups: after the first year, combine NBVs and calculate together.

C. When Does Depreciation Start?

Rule

Depreciation starts when the NCA is available for use — not ordered, not delivered, but ready to use (e.g., assembly/testing complete).

Ordered 1 Jan → arrived 1 Feb → assembled & tested by 1 March → paid 1 April.

Start date: 1 March. For year end 31 Dec → 10/12 of annual dep.

D. Fully Depreciated NCAs

Rule

Once NBV reaches the residual value → stop depreciating. Asset stays on books at residual value until disposal.

Double Entry for Depreciation

Annual Depreciation Entry
DateAccountDrCr
Dec 31Depreciation: NCA [Expense → I/S]X

Dr: Depreciation: NCA

Expense account → I/S. This year's cost only. Closed to P&L at year end.

Cr: Acc. Dep: NCA

Contra-asset → SFP. Accumulates year after year. Balance carries forward.

Why not credit the asset directly? Because we want to keep the original cost visible. Showing Cost − Acc Dep = NBV gives users more information.

13 ADVANCED

Improvements to Existing NCAs

When capital expenditure is made on an existing NCA (improvement, not just repair):

  1. Add the improvement cost to the NCA
  2. Recalculate depreciation for the remaining useful life
After Improvement (Straight-Line)
New annual dep = (NBV after improvement − Revised residual value) ÷ Remaining useful life
Where NBV after improvement = old NBV + cost of improvement

Example 1: Extending Useful Life

Motor van $60,000. 1 Jan 2021. SL. Life: 4 yrs. Residual: $10,000. Dep = ($60K−$10K)÷4 = $12,500/yr

On 1 Jan 2023, $5,000 spent on enhancement. Residual stays $10,000. Life extended by 1 year.

YearCalculationDepCostAcc DepNBV
2021Normal$12,500$60,000$12,500$47,500
2022Normal$12,500$60,000$25,000$35,000
2023($35K+$5K−$10K)÷(4−2+1)$10,000$65,000$35,000$30,000
2024Same$10,000$65,000$45,000$20,000
2025Same$10,000$65,000$55,000$10,000 ✓

How the 2023 depreciation works:

  • NBV before improvement = $35,000
  • + Improvement = $5,000 → NBV after = $40,000
  • Revised residual = $10,000 (unchanged)
  • Remaining life = original 4 − 2 used + 1 extended = 3 years
  • New dep = ($40,000 − $10,000) ÷ 3 = $10,000

Example 2: Change in Residual + Extended Life

Same van. On 1 Jan 2023, $5,000 spent. Residual decreased by $2,500 (now $7,500). Life extended by 2 years.

New dep = ($35,000 + $5,000 − $7,500) ÷ (4 − 2 + 2) = $32,500 ÷ 4 = $8,125

NBV at end of 2026 = $65,000 − $25,000 − $8,125×4 = $7,500 ✓ (= new residual)

Example 3: No Change in Residual or Life

Same van. On 1 Jan 2023, $5,000 spent. Remaining life and residual unchanged.

New dep = ($35,000 + $5,000 − $10,000) ÷ (4 − 2) = $30,000 ÷ 2 = $15,000

Shortcut (only when no change in RV or remaining life): treat improvement as a separate NCA with the same remaining life:

$12,500 (existing) + $5,000 ÷ 2 (new) = $15,000 ✓

14 DISPOSAL

Disposal of Non-Current Assets

A business may sell an NCA before, at, or after the end of its useful life. The key idea:

Core Concept

Profit on disposal = Proceeds > NBV → goes to I/S as other revenue
Loss on disposal = Proceeds < NBV → goes to I/S as expense

NBV at disposal = Cost − Acc. Depreciation to disposal date

We use a temporary Disposal account to bring everything together.

4 Steps of Disposal

StepWhatDrCr
1Transfer cost outDisposalNCA (at cost)
2Transfer acc dep outAcc dep: NCADisposal
3Record proceedsCash/Bank/ReceivableDisposal
4Balance = profit or lossLoss on disposal ORDisposal
DisposalProfit on disposal

Worked Example

Motor van cost $60,000. Bought 1 Jul 2018. Life: 4 years. Scrap: $12,000. SL. Year end: 31 Dec.

Annual dep = ($60K − $12K) ÷ 4 = $12,000

Acc dep at 1 Jan 2020 = $12,000 × 1.5 years = $18,000. NBV = $42,000.

Key Formula
Cash paid = Cost of new NCA − Trade-in value of old NCA
