<h2>Non-Current Assets</h2>


<p>Your bakery buys <strong>flour</strong> — it's gone within a week. It also buys an <strong>oven</strong> — that stays for years. The oven is a <strong>non-current asset</strong>.</p>


<div class="kc">

<div class="tg">Definition</div>

<p><strong>Non-current assets (NCAs)</strong> = assets held <strong>longer than one year</strong> to help the business earn money. They are <em>not</em> intended to be turned into cash quickly.</p>

</div>


<p><strong>Examples:</strong> 🚐 Motor vehicles · 🏢 Premises · 🖥️ Equipment · ⚙️ Machinery · 🪑 Furniture</p>


<p>The big question: if an NCA helps you earn money for <em>5 years</em>, should you record its entire cost as an expense in Year 1? That feels unfair. This is why we need <strong>depreciation</strong> — but first, let's figure out what "cost" actually means.</p>


<h3>What Goes Into the "Cost" of an NCA?</h3>


<p>Under the <strong>historical cost principle</strong>, assets are recorded at their <strong>actual acquisition cost</strong>. But not every dollar spent <em>around</em> an asset counts. We split all spending into two categories:</p>


<div class="qz" id="q1a">

<div class="ql">Quick Check</div>

<p class="qt">A bakery buys a delivery van for $80,000. Is this a non-current asset?</p>

<div class="sec-lab"><span class="n">2</span> FOUNDATIONS</div>

<h2>Capital vs Revenue Expenditure</h2>


<div class="cg">

<div class="cc a">

<h4>💎 Capital Expenditure 資本支出</h4>

<p><strong>Long-term benefit.</strong> Added to the NCA cost on the SFP.</p>

<p><em>"This spending creates or improves something lasting."</em></p>

</div>

<div class="cc b">

<h4>🔥 Revenue Expenditure 收益支出</h4>

<p><strong>Short-term benefit only.</strong> Expensed in the Income Statement now.</p>

<p><em>"This spending is used up — it won't benefit next year."</em></p>

</div>

</div>


<h3>Case 1: Buying a NEW NCA</h3>

<p><strong>Capital</strong> = purchase price + anything needed to bring the NCA to its <strong>location and condition for use</strong>.</p>


<div class="kc">

<div class="tg">The Golden Rule</div>

<p>Ask: <strong>"Without this spending, could the asset actually be used?"</strong><br>NO → Capital. YES → Revenue.</p>

</div>


<p><strong>Revenue</strong> clues: repeating payments (annual insurance), consumables (petrol, electricity).</p>


<div class="cd or">

<h2>Why Depreciation?</h2>


<p>You buy a van for <strong>$60,000</strong>, useful for 5 years. If you expense it all in Year 1:</p>

<ul>

<li>Year 1: profit looks terrible (huge expense, but van only helped <em>part</em> of the time)</li>

<li>Years 2–5: you're using the van for free in your accounts. Not fair!</li>

</ul>


<div class="kc">

<div class="tg">Matching Principle 相配原則</div>

<p>Expenses must be matched to the <strong>same period</strong> as the revenue they generate. Van helps earn money for 5 years → spread its cost over 5 years.</p>

</div>


<div class="kc">

<div class="tg">Definition</div>

<p><strong>Depreciation</strong> = the <strong>systematic allocation</strong> of the depreciable amount of an NCA over its estimated useful life.</p>

<p><em>Plain English: spreading the "used up" cost across all the years the asset works for you.</em></p>

</div>


<h3>What Causes It?</h3>

<ol>

<li><strong>Physical wear &amp; tear</strong> — gets old from use</li>

<li><strong>Obsolescence</strong> — new technology makes it outdated</li>

<li><strong>Inadequacy</strong> — business grows, asset is no longer enough</li>

</ol>


<div class="tip">Depreciation ≠ market value drop. A building may rise in market value, but we still depreciate it — we're allocating cost, not tracking value.</div>

</section>

<h2>Straight-Line Method</h2>


<div class="kc"><div class="tg">Concept</div>

<p>Asset generates benefits <strong>evenly</strong> → charge the <strong>same depreciation every year</strong>. Like cutting a pizza into equal slices.</p></div>


<div class="fm"><div class="lb">Formulas</div>

<strong>Annual Dep = (Cost − Residual Value) ÷ Useful Life</strong><br>

<strong>Annual Dep = (Cost − Residual Value) × Rate</strong><br>

<span style="color:var(--o300)">Rate = 100% ÷ Useful Life (e.g. 5 years → 20%)</span>

<h2>Reducing-Balance Method</h2>


<div class="kc"><div class="tg">Concept</div>

<p>Apply the rate to the <strong>net book value</strong> (not cost) each year. Since NBV shrinks, depreciation gets <strong>smaller every year</strong>.</p></div>


<div class="fm"><div class="lb">Formula</div>

<strong>Depreciation = (Cost − Acc. Dep.) × Rate = NBV × Rate</strong>

</div>


<div class="trap">

<p><strong>IGNORE the residual value in the calculation.</strong> Just apply rate × NBV. The residual value is NOT subtracted from cost first (unlike straight-line).</p>

</div>


<div class="tip">Exam clue: "X% on <strong>net book value</strong>" or "on <strong>reducing/diminishing balance</strong>" = this method.</div>

<div class="sec-lab"><span class="n">13</span> ADVANCED</div>

<h2>Improvements to Existing NCAs</h2>


<p>When <strong>capital expenditure</strong> is made on an existing NCA (improvement, not just repair):</p>

<ol>

<li><strong>Add</strong> the improvement cost to the NCA</li>

<li><strong>Recalculate</strong> depreciation for the remaining useful life</li>

</ol>


<div class="fm"><div class="lb">After Improvement (Straight-Line)</div>

<strong>New annual dep = (NBV after improvement − Revised residual value) ÷ Remaining useful life</strong><br>

<span style="color:var(--o300)">Where NBV after improvement = old NBV + cost of improvement</span>

</div>


<h3>Example 1: Extending Useful Life</h3>

<div class="cd or">

<p>Motor van $60,000. 1 Jan 2021. SL. Life: 4 yrs. Residual: $10,000. Dep = ($60K−$10K)÷4 = <strong>$12,500/yr</strong></p>

<p>On <strong>1 Jan 2023</strong>, $5,000 spent on enhancement. Residual stays $10,000. Life <strong>extended by 1 year</strong>.</p>

</div>


<table class="dt">

<thead><tr><th>Year</th><th>Calculation</th><th>Dep</th><th>Cost</th><th>Acc Dep</th><th>NBV</th></tr></thead>

<tbody>

<tr><td>2021</td><td>Normal</td><td class="ar">$12,500</td><td class="ar">$60,000</td><td class="ar">$12,500</td><td class="ar">$47,500</td></tr>

<tr><td>2022</td><td>Normal</td><td class="ar">$12,500</td><td class="ar">$60,000</td><td class="ar">$25,000</td><td class="ar">$35,000</td></tr>

<tr><td>2023</td><td style="font-size:.8rem">($35K+$5K−$10K)÷(4−2+1)</td><td class="ar"><strong>$10,000</strong></td><td class="ar">$65,000</td><td class="ar">$35,000</td><td class="ar">$30,000</td></tr>

<tr><td>2024</td><td>Same</td><td class="ar">$10,000</td><td class="ar">$65,000</td><td class="ar">$45,000</td><td class="ar">$20,000</td></tr>

<tr><td>2025</td><td>Same</td><td class="ar">$10,000</td><td class="ar">$65,000</td><td class="ar">$55,000</td><td class="ar">$10,000 ✓</td></tr>

</tbody>

</table>


<p><strong>How the 2023 depreciation works:</strong></p>

<ul>

<li>NBV before improvement = $35,000</li>

<li>+ Improvement = $5,000 → NBV after = $40,000</li>

<li>Revised residual = $10,000 (unchanged)</li>

<li>Remaining life = original 4 − 2 used + 1 extended = <strong>3 years</strong></li>

<li>New dep = ($40,000 − $10,000) ÷ 3 = <strong>$10,000</strong></li>

</ul>


<h3>Example 2: Change in Residual + Extended Life</h3>

<div class="cd or">

<p>Same van. On 1 Jan 2023, $5,000 spent. Residual <strong>decreased by $2,500</strong> (now $7,500). Life <strong>extended by 2 years</strong>.</p>

</div>

<p>New dep = ($35,000 + $5,000 − $7,500) ÷ (4 − 2 + 2) = $32,500 ÷ 4 = <strong>$8,125</strong></p>

<p>NBV at end of 2026 = $65,000 − $25,000 − $8,125×4 = <strong>$7,500</strong> ✓ (= new residual)</p>


<h3>Example 3: No Change in Residual or Life</h3>

<div class="cd or">

<p>Same van. On 1 Jan 2023, $5,000 spent. Remaining life and residual <strong>unchanged</strong>.</p>

</div>

<p>New dep = ($35,000 + $5,000 − $10,000) ÷ (4 − 2) = $30,000 ÷ 2 = <strong>$15,000</strong></p>


<div class="tip">

<p><strong>Shortcut</strong> (only when no change in RV or remaining life): treat improvement as a separate NCA with the same remaining life:</p>

<p>$12,500 (existing) + $5,000 ÷ 2 (new) = <strong>$15,000</strong> ✓</p>

</div>

<div class="fm"><div class="lb">Key Formula</div>

<strong>Cash paid = Cost of new NCA − Trade-in value of old NCA</strong>

</div>


<h3>4 Steps</h3>

<table class="dt">

<thead><tr><th>Step</th><th>Dr</th><th>Cr</th></tr></thead>

<tbody>

<tr><td>2. Transfer old AD</td><td>Acc dep</td><td>Disposal</td></tr>

</tbody>

</table>


<h3>Worked Example</h3>

<div class="cd or">
